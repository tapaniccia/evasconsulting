Part 1

Why SOPs Matter

(and why most founders skip them)

A Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) is a written, step-by-step set of instructions for how a specific task gets done. Nothing fancy — just clear enough that someone other than you could follow it and get the same result.

Most founders know they should have SOPs. Almost none do. Here's why that costs more than it seems:

Without SOPs

  • Every new hire learns by shadowing someone — slow, inconsistent, and dependent on whoever's training them that day.
  • Quality varies depending on who does the task.
  • You're the institutional memory. If you're out, the process stalls or breaks.
  • The same mistakes get repeated because nothing was written down the first time.

With SOPs

  • New hires get productive faster, with less of your time spent training.
  • Tasks get done the same way regardless of who's doing them.
  • You can delegate with confidence instead of anxiety.
  • Mistakes get fixed once — in the document — instead of repeatedly in real life.
The 30-Minute Rule

You don't need to document everything at once. Pick one process that you or your team repeats weekly, and that causes friction when it's done inconsistently. Document just that one first. Momentum matters more than completeness.

Part 2

The SOP Writing Guide

Follow these five steps to turn any process living in your head (or your best employee's head) into a usable SOP.

1

Do the task yourself, and take notes as you go

Don't write an SOP from memory. Either perform the task while narrating each click and decision, or shadow whoever does it best and write down exactly what they do — including the small judgment calls they make without thinking.

2

Break it into numbered steps

Each step should be one action. If a single line in your notes contains the word "and," it's probably two steps. Aim for steps specific enough that someone with no context could follow them without guessing.

3

Add the details that prevent mistakes

This is what separates a real SOP from a vague outline. For each step, ask:

  • What tool, login, or system is needed?
  • What does 'done correctly' look like for this step?
  • What's the most common mistake made here, and how do you avoid it?
4

Define the trigger and the outcome

State clearly what kicks off this process (a new lead comes in, a client signs a contract, it's the first of the month) and what the finished result should look like when the process is complete.

5

Test it on someone who's never done the task

Hand the SOP to a teammate who doesn't normally do this task and watch them follow it without help. Anywhere they hesitate, ask a question, or get it wrong is a gap in the document — not a gap in them. Fix the SOP, not the person.

Common Mistake

Writing SOPs that are too vague ("reach out to the client") or too rigid (scripting every word). Aim for clear enough to remove guesswork, flexible enough to allow judgment where it's actually needed.

Part 5

Example SOP (Filled In)

Here's the same template completed for a real process, so you can see what a finished SOP actually looks like.

+
Process Name
New Client Onboarding
Department / Area
Client Services
Owner
Operations Manager
Last Updated
June 2026
Review Frequency
Every 90 days
Purpose
Ensures every new client has a consistent, professional first experience and has everything needed to begin work without delay.
Trigger
A new client signs the service agreement and pays the initial deposit.
Tools & Access Needed
  • CRM (client record creation)
  • Email platform (welcome sequence)
  • Project management tool (project setup)
  • Shared client folder (Google Drive)
Step-by-Step Instructions
  1. Create a new client record in the CRM with contact details and signed agreement attached.
  2. Send the Welcome Email within 24 hours of contract signing, using the standard template.
  3. Create a new project in the project management tool using the onboarding project template.
  4. Create a shared client folder and share access with the client and assigned team members.
  5. Send the Discovery Questionnaire and request it be completed within 5 business days.
  6. Schedule the Kickoff Call once the questionnaire is returned.
  7. Confirm internally which team member is the client's main point of contact and notify the client.
Common Mistakes to Avoid
  • Sending the Welcome Email before the deposit has actually cleared — confirm payment first.
  • Forgetting to assign folder permissions, which delays the client's ability to share files.
  • Scheduling the Kickoff Call before the Discovery Questionnaire is returned, which leads to an unfocused first meeting.
Definition of Done
Client has received the Welcome Email, has access to their shared folder, has returned the Discovery Questionnaire, and the Kickoff Call is on the calendar — all within 7 business days of signing.
